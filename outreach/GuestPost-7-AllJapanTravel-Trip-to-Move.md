## TITRE
Dreaming of Moving to Japan After Your Trip? A Practical First Guide

---

## ARTICLE

It happens on almost every trip to Japan. Somewhere around day five, watching the city glide past a train window, the thought arrives: what if I did not go home? Plenty of travellers have that daydream. A few turn it into a plan. If you are in the second group, here is an honest, practical first guide to turning "I love visiting" into "I live here."

Start with the visa, because everything hangs on it

You cannot simply move to Japan the way you extend a holiday. You need a status of residence, and for most people that means a job that sponsors a work visa, a student visa for language or university study, or one of the newer routes like the digital nomad or working holiday visa if you qualify. Figure out which path fits you before anything else, because your visa shapes how long you can stay and what you are allowed to do.

Budget for the setup, not just the flight

Travellers budget for a trip. Movers need to budget for a landing. Beyond the flight and shipping, the big surprise is housing: a standard Japanese lease often asks for several months of rent upfront, combining a deposit, key money, agency and guarantor fees. Many newcomers start in a share house or furnished apartment, which need far less cash upfront and no Japanese guarantor, then move into a regular lease once they are settled. Knowing this in advance turns a nasty shock into a simple plan.

Pick your city with clear eyes

The Japan you love as a tourist is not always the Japan that suits daily life. Tokyo has the most international jobs and services but higher rents and a faster pace. Osaka is cheaper and famously friendly. Smaller cities offer space and calm but fewer English-speaking opportunities. Match the city to your work and your temperament, not just to your favourite holiday memory.

Sort the boring essentials early

Once you arrive, a short checklist unlocks normal life: register your address at the ward office, get your residence card, open a bank account, sign up for health insurance, and get a local phone plan. It feels bureaucratic, but each step is what turns you from a long visitor into an actual resident, and doing them quickly makes everything else easier.

Learn a little of the language

You can travel Japan on a translation app. Living here is smoother with some Japanese, even just the basics for daily errands, forms and conversations with a landlord or neighbour. You do not need to be fluent to be happy, but a little effort goes a long way toward feeling at home rather than perpetually lost in the fine print.

The daydream is doable

Moving to Japan is not a fantasy reserved for the lucky few. Thousands of people do it every year, most without speaking much Japanese at the start. The difference between the travellers who dream about it and the ones who actually go is simple: preparation. Sort the visa, budget honestly for the setup, choose your city with your head as well as your heart, and give yourself patience. The country that captivated you on holiday is even better when it becomes home.
