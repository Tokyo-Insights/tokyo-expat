## TITRE
Setting Up Home in Tokyo: A First-Timer's Timeline from Landing to Keys

---

## ARTICLE

When I moved to Tokyo, I assumed the apartment would be the easy part. I was wrong, and the surprise was not the city but the process. Looking back, what I wish I had was a simple timeline: what happens, in what order, and how long each step really takes. So here is the honest version, from the day you land to the day you get your keys.

Week zero: before you even arrive

The single best thing you can do happens before you land: line up temporary housing for your first two to four weeks. Almost nobody signs a standard Japanese lease from abroad, and you do not want to be apartment-hunting while jet-lagged and homeless. A short-term furnished flat, a share house, or even a modest hotel buys you breathing room. I also recommend arriving with a data SIM sorted, because you will be online constantly, comparing listings and messaging agents.

Days one to three: the paperwork foundation

Your first mission on the ground is not the apartment, it is the paperwork that unlocks it. Register your address at the local ward office and get your residence card business in order. Then open a Japanese bank account, because so much of daily life, rent included, runs on automatic local payments. These two steps feel bureaucratic and slow, but almost every landlord and guarantor company will ask for them, so the sooner they are done, the sooner you can apply for a real apartment.

Week one: define your search honestly

Before viewing anything, I sat down and answered three questions: what is my realistic monthly budget, where do I actually need to commute, and how much upfront cash do I have. That last one matters more than people expect, because a standard lease can ask for four to six months of rent all at once. Once I was honest about those numbers, the map of Tokyo shrank from overwhelming to manageable, and I could focus on the few train lines and wards that actually fit.

Weeks one to two: viewings and the guarantor reality

This is where the Japanese system surprised me most. Landlords almost always want a guarantor, and as a newcomer I did not have a Japanese person to play that role. The solution, which nobody had explained to me, is a guarantor company: for a fee they stand in as your guarantor, and using one is completely normal. Once I understood that, applications stopped feeling like rejections waiting to happen. I viewed a handful of places, learned to read the layout codes, and kept a short list rather than falling for the first decent flat.

The application: expect a few days

When you find the apartment, you apply, and then you wait. The application goes to the landlord and the guarantor company for screening, which usually takes a few days. Have your documents ready in advance to speed this up: residence card, passport, proof of income or an employment letter, and your bank details. A hesitant landlord is reassured far more by a complete, organised application than by anything you say, so this preparation genuinely changes your odds.

Signing day: the bill and the keys

Approval is a relief, and then comes the contract. This is when you pay the upfront costs: the deposit, key money, agency fee, guarantor fee, insurance and first month of rent. Read each line so nothing is a surprise, sign, and finally, the keys are yours. From landing to keys, the whole thing realistically takes three to six weeks, longer than most people plan for, which is exactly why that first bit of temporary housing is so important.

What I would tell my past self

Three things. First, treat the apartment search as its own project with its own timeline, not something you squeeze in after arriving. Second, do the boring paperwork first, because it unlocks everything else. Third, do not take rejections personally; some landlords simply opt out of foreign tenants, and the fix is to focus on foreigner-friendly listings and lean on a guarantor company. Tokyo rewards preparation, and once the keys are in your hand, daily life here is smoother and more rewarding than almost anywhere I have lived.
