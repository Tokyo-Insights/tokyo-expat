## TITRE
Living in Tokyo as an Expat: An Honest Guide to Finding a Home, Neighbourhoods and What It Really Costs

---

## ARTICLE

When I first decided to move to Tokyo, I thought the hard part would be the language. It turned out the hardest part was finding somewhere to live. Nobody warned me that renting an apartment here works nothing like it does back home, and I made a few expensive mistakes before I figured it out. So if you are planning your own move, here is the honest version of what living in Tokyo is actually like, starting with the thing that stresses everyone out the most: getting a home.

### The guarantor system nobody warns you about

In most countries you find a flat, you pay a deposit, you sign, you move in. In Japan there is an extra character in the story: the guarantor. Landlords here almost always want someone (or something) that promises to cover your rent if you cannot. Traditionally that was a Japanese person with a stable job who vouched for you. As a foreigner who just arrived, you usually do not have that person.

The good news is that guarantor companies exist precisely to solve this. You pay them a fee (often around half a month to one month of rent up front, plus a small yearly renewal) and they play the role of your guarantor. Most foreigner-friendly listings expect you to use one, and it is completely normal. The mistake I made early on was applying to apartments that quietly did not accept foreign tenants at all, and wondering why I kept getting rejected. It was not personal. Some landlords simply opt out, and knowing that saved me a lot of frustration.

### The move-in bill that made me sit down

Here is the number that shocks most newcomers. To move into a standard Japanese lease, you do not just pay a deposit. You often pay a stack of one-time costs that, added together, can reach four to six months of rent before you have spent a single night there.

The usual suspects are:

- **Shikikin (deposit):** usually one to two months, partly refundable.
- **Reikin (key money):** often one to two months, and this one is a gift to the landlord. You never see it again.
- **Agency fee:** typically one month plus tax.
- **Guarantor company fee:** as mentioned above.
- **First month rent, insurance, lock change,** and a few smaller extras.

When I realised the upfront total, I completely changed my strategy. If you are arriving with a fixed budget, this bill matters more than the monthly rent itself. One tip that genuinely helps: look for apartments advertised as "reikin zero." They exist, and skipping key money alone can save you a month or two of rent.

### Neighbourhoods: where I would actually tell a friend to look

Tokyo is enormous, and the "best" area completely depends on what you want your daily life to feel like. Here is how I would break it down for a friend.

If you want the polished, international, embassy-and-cafe version of Tokyo, look at **Hiroo, Azabu and Minato**. It is beautiful and convenient, and it is also the most expensive part of the city, so go in with your eyes open.

If you want style and a younger, design-conscious crowd, **Ebisu, Daikanyama and Nakameguro** are hard to beat. Great food, walkable streets, still central, and slightly less brutal on the wallet than the embassy district.

If you want more space for your money and a calmer, more residential feel, look further out into wards like **Setagaya, Nakano or Koto**. Your commute gets a bit longer, but the same budget suddenly buys a real apartment instead of a shoebox.

The single best piece of advice I can give: do not choose your neighbourhood before you choose your commute. In Tokyo, ten extra minutes on the right train line changes both your rent and your quality of life more than the postcode does.

### What it really costs each month

People always ask me for one magic number, and there is not one, because layout and location swing the price enormously. But to give you a realistic sense, based on the actual rental market:

- A compact one-room or 1K apartment (think studio) runs roughly 75,000 yen in the outer wards and climbs toward 130,000 to 140,000 yen in the central premium areas.
- A one-bedroom 1LDK, which is what a couple or someone who wants proper space usually targets, ranges from around 120,000 yen out from the centre to 250,000 yen and up in the most sought-after districts.

On top of rent, budget for utilities, a phone plan, and transport. The train system is fantastic and, if you commute the same route daily, a season pass is very cost-effective and often reimbursed by employers. Food can be as cheap or as expensive as you let it, and honestly the cheap end here is delicious.

### The things I wish I had known on day one

A few hard-won lessons, quickly:

- **Open a bank account early.** So much of daily life, from rent to utilities, runs on automatic local payments.
- **Keep your residence card on you.** You will need it constantly for the first few weeks of setting things up.
- **Home internet takes two to four weeks to install.** Get a data SIM at the airport so you are not offline while you wait.
- **Move light.** Japanese apartments are smaller than you expect, and buying locally is often simpler than shipping your old life across the ocean.

### Would I do it again? Absolutely

For all the paperwork and the culture shock of the rental system, living in Tokyo has been one of the best decisions of my life. It is safe, it is endlessly interesting, the food alone is worth the move, and once you crack the housing puzzle, daily life here is smoother than almost anywhere I have been. The trick is simply to go in prepared, treat the search as its own project, and not take the rejections personally. If I could do it with a few mistakes and no Japanese, you can absolutely do it too.
