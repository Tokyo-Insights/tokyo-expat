## TITRE
From Visitor to Resident: What Really Changes When You Move to Japan

---

## ARTICLE

Almost everyone who moves to Japan first came as a visitor. You fall for the trains that run on time, the food, the quiet politeness, and somewhere between a bowl of ramen and a temple garden you think: I could live here. Then you actually do it, and you discover that living in Japan is a different country from visiting it. Here is what really changes when you cross that line.

The paperwork becomes your new hobby

As a tourist, Japan asks almost nothing of you. As a resident, it asks for everything in triplicate. You register your address at the ward office, get a residence card you must carry at all times, open a bank account, and sign up for national health insurance and pension. None of it is hard, but it is a rhythm of forms and small offices that no amount of holidays prepared you for. The upside: once it is done, daily life runs on rails.

Housing is the real culture shock

Tourists see Japan through hotel windows. Residents meet the rental market, and it is nothing like home. Landlords often want a guarantor, many charge non-refundable key money on top of a deposit, and the upfront cost of a standard lease can reach several months of rent before you move in. The workaround most newcomers eventually learn is the guarantor company, which stands in for the Japanese guarantor you do not have. Understanding this early saves a lot of confusion, because the apartment, not the visa, is often the hardest part of settling in.

Convenience is real, but so is the fine print

The convenience you loved as a visitor is even better as a resident. Everything is close, safe, and reliable. But you also meet the fine print: rubbish sorted into many categories on fixed days, apartments smaller than the photos suggested, and a cash-and-paperwork culture hiding under the high-tech surface. It is charming and occasionally maddening, sometimes in the same afternoon.

Language stops being optional, gently

On a trip, you get by with a translation app and goodwill. Living here, the app still helps, but the small frictions add up: a letter from the city, a form at the bank, a conversation with a landlord. You do not need fluency to live well, and many foreigners thrive with modest Japanese, but you will want to learn enough to handle the everyday. It is less about survival and more about feeling at home.

The community shifts

As a visitor you are always passing through. As a resident you start to build something: a local coffee shop that knows your order, neighbours who nod, a routine. The expat community is real and welcoming, but the deeper reward is slowly becoming a regular somewhere, which is a feeling no holiday can give you.

Would I recommend making the leap?

Wholeheartedly, with one caveat: go in prepared, not starry-eyed. Treat the move as a project. Sort the paperwork first, budget honestly for the housing bill, learn a little language, and give yourself a few months of patience while the new country becomes ordinary life. The Japan you loved as a visitor is still there when you live here. It just comes wrapped in a residence card and a recycling schedule, and somehow that makes it even more yours.
