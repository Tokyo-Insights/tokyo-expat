## TITRE
Understanding the Japanese Rental Contract: A Foreigner's Guide to Key Money, Deposits and Fees

---

## ARTICLE

For most foreigners, the biggest surprise when renting in Japan is not the apartments themselves but the contract. A Japanese lease comes with a set of one-time payments and recurring fees that simply do not exist in many countries, and the total can add up to several months of rent before you have even received the keys. Understanding each line before you sign will save you from an unpleasant shock and help you budget properly. Here is what a Japanese rental contract actually contains.

### Shikikin: the deposit

Shikikin is the security deposit, usually equal to one or two months of rent. As in most countries, it is held against damage and unpaid rent, and part of it is refundable when you leave. In practice, cleaning and minor repair costs are often deducted, so do not count on getting all of it back. It is the most familiar item on the list for newcomers.

### Reikin: key money

Reikin, or key money, is the item that surprises people the most. It is typically one or two months of rent, and unlike the deposit, it is a non-refundable gift to the landlord. You receive nothing tangible in return, and you never see the money again. It is a cultural leftover from a time when housing was scarce, and while it is slowly disappearing, it remains common. The good news is that many listings are now advertised as reikin zero, meaning no key money is charged, and choosing one of these can save you a full month or two of rent instantly.

### The agency fee

Most rentals are arranged through a real estate agency, and the agency charges a brokerage fee, usually one month of rent plus consumption tax. This is a standard cost of using an agent to find and process the apartment, and it is paid once at the start.

### The guarantor company fee

Japanese landlords almost always require a guarantor, someone who promises to cover the rent if the tenant cannot. Since most foreigners do not have a Japanese citizen able to play this role, guarantor companies fill the gap. They charge an initial fee, often between half a month and one month of rent, plus a smaller annual renewal fee. Using one is completely normal and is usually the simplest path to approval for a foreign tenant.

### Insurance, lock changes and other small costs

A few smaller items round out the bill. Tenants are generally required to buy fire and household insurance, typically a modest fixed sum for a two-year term. Landlords often charge a lock-change fee so that the new tenant has fresh keys, and some properties add a cleaning fee. Individually these are small, but together they add to the upfront total.

### Koshinryo: the renewal fee

One recurring cost that catches people off guard appears later. Most Japanese leases run for two years, and when you renew, many landlords charge a renewal fee, or koshinryo, often equal to one month of rent. If you plan to stay several years, factor this into your longer-term budget, because it returns every couple of years.

### The real upfront total

When you add the deposit, key money, agency fee, guarantor fee, insurance and the first month of rent, the initial payment on a standard lease commonly reaches four to six months of rent. For a foreigner arriving with a fixed relocation budget, this upfront figure often matters more than the monthly rent itself, and it should be the first number you plan around.

### How to reduce the burden

There are practical ways to soften the cost. Target reikin zero listings to skip key money. Ask whether the agency fee or key money can be negotiated, as there is sometimes room, especially on properties that have been vacant. Consider furnished apartments or share houses as a first step, since many require far lower upfront payments and no guarantor, giving you time to build a financial cushion before moving into a standard lease.

### Read before you sign

None of these costs are hidden or improper; they are simply how the Japanese rental market works. The mistake foreigners make is not knowing about them until the contract is in front of them. Once you understand what shikikin, reikin, the agency fee, the guarantor fee and koshinryo are, you can budget accurately, compare listings intelligently, and sign your Japanese lease with confidence rather than surprise.
