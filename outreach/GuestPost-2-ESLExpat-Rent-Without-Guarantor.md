## TITRE
How English Teachers Can Rent an Apartment in Japan Without a Guarantor

---

## ARTICLE

If you have just landed a teaching job in Japan, congratulations. Now comes the part that catches almost every new teacher off guard: finding a place to live. You would think that with a signed contract in hand, renting an apartment would be simple. Instead, many teachers hit a wall the moment a landlord asks for a guarantor. Here is how that system works, and exactly how to get your own apartment even when you do not have one.

### Why teachers get stuck

Most Japanese landlords will not rent to you unless someone guarantees your rent. Traditionally that guarantor is a Japanese citizen with a stable income who agrees to cover you if you fall behind. As a teacher who just arrived, you almost certainly do not know anyone who fits that description.

On top of that, teachers face a few extra hurdles that landlords quietly worry about: you are new to the country, you have no local credit history, your visa is tied to a single employer, and there may be a language barrier during the application. None of this means you are a bad tenant. It just means the standard rental process was not built with you in mind.

### The fix: guarantor companies

Here is the good news. You do not actually need a human guarantor anymore. Guarantor companies exist specifically to solve this, and using one is completely normal in Japan today.

For a fee, usually around half a month to one month of rent up front plus a small annual renewal, the company acts as your guarantor. The landlord gets their security, and you get the apartment. When you look at listings, many will already state which guarantor company they work with. This is the single most important thing to understand as a teacher: you are not disqualified by the guarantor requirement, you just route around it with a company instead of a person.

### Options that make life easier for new teachers

Depending on how much certainty and speed you want, you have a few routes:

**1. Company or school housing.** Many eikaiwa chains and JET-style placements offer subsidised apartments or help arrange one. It is often not the cheapest or nicest option, but it removes the guarantor headache entirely and gets you a roof on day one. Great for your first few months while you find your feet.

**2. Guarantor company plus a standard lease.** This is the normal route once you are settled. You pick your own apartment on the open market and use a guarantor company. You get full choice, but you also pay the full set of Japanese move-in costs (more on that below).

**3. Share houses and foreigner-friendly furnished apartments.** These are the fastest and lowest-barrier option. Many accept tenants with no guarantor at all, come furnished, and let you apply online before you even arrive. Rent is often all-inclusive. For a teacher arriving with limited savings, this is frequently the smartest first move.

### Budget for the upfront costs, not just the rent

The part that surprises teachers the most is not the monthly rent, it is the move-in bill. A standard Japanese lease can ask for four to six months of rent all at once before you move in, made up of a deposit, key money (a non-refundable gift to the landlord), the agency fee, the guarantor fee, and first month rent.

Two practical tips that save real money:

- Look for listings marked **reikin zero** (no key money). Skipping key money alone can save you a month or two of rent.
- If your savings are thin when you arrive, start with a share house or furnished apartment, then move into a standard lease once you have built up a cushion and a bit of local footing.

### Documents to have ready

Landlords and guarantor companies will usually want to see your residence card, your passport, your employment contract or a letter from your school, and sometimes a bank statement. Having these organised before you start viewing apartments makes the whole process dramatically faster, and it reassures a hesitant landlord that you are a reliable tenant.

### A quick word on rejections

If an application gets turned down, try not to take it personally. Some landlords simply opt out of renting to foreign tenants, and that is about their policy, not about you. The trick is to focus your search on foreigner-friendly listings and, ideally, to have someone who can communicate with the agency in Japanese. When the landlord feels reassured, approvals come much more easily.

### You can absolutely do this

The guarantor system looks intimidating from the outside, but thousands of teachers navigate it every single year, most of them without speaking much Japanese. Understand that you route around the guarantor with a company, budget for the upfront costs, keep your documents ready, and start with a lower-barrier option if you need speed. Do that, and you will have your own place in Japan sooner than you think, and you can get on with the actual reason you came: teaching, and enjoying one of the most rewarding countries in the world to live in.
